Goal: learn V and Φ from unlabeled snapshots — without knowing which particle is which

The Setup

What are V and Φ?

Three particles under two forces. The blue arrows pull each particle toward the center (confinement V). The purple arrows push particles apart (interaction Φ). The gray arrows are random noise. The combined effect produces the complex motion you see.

We observe the particle positions, but we don't know V or Φ. Our goal: reverse-engineer these forces from the data alone.

The Problem

The Problem: Unlabeled Data

Imagine photographing a flock of birds at two different times. Each snapshot shows where every bird is — but you can't tell which bird is which across photos. Even with just 4 particles, there are 4! = 24 possible matchings. In real experiments with N=100, that's 100! ≈ 10158 — more than the atoms in the universe.

Toggle to see what information is lost
Left: labeled snapshot. Right: same particles, unknown identity. Which matching is correct?

Without labels, we cannot match particles across snapshots — and we don't try to. Our method works directly with the empirical distribution, bypassing the N! combinatorial explosion entirely.

The Challenge

Why Existing Methods Fail

Traditional methods estimate velocity as v ≈ ΔX/Δt. This creates two fatal problems: when Δt is small, noise explodes (∝ σ/√Δt); when Δt is large, discretization bias grows as O(Δt) and Sinkhorn matching accuracy drops to random chance. Drag the slider to see the collapse:

Labeled MLE: Noise Explosion

Estimates velocity as v ≈ ΔX/Δt. When Δt → 0, noise ∝ σ/√Δt → ∞. When Δt is large, O(Δt) discretization bias accumulates over N−1 interaction terms.

∇V: 0.8% → 11.3% as Δt grows

Sinkhorn MLE: Matching Collapse

Uses optimal transport to guess which particle is which. At small Δt, works well (particles barely move). At large Δt, particles mix completely — matching accuracy drops to 24.5%, near the 10% random baseline.

∇Φ: 0.8% → 95.8% as Δt grows

Our self-test avoids both pitfalls: no velocity estimation, no trajectory matching

Self-Test ∇V: 0.67% → 6.84% (7× more robust than Sinkhorn at large Δt)

Our Approach

From Particles to Distributions: The Weak Form PDE

Key realization: we don't need to track individual particles. The empirical distribution μₚ = (1/N)∑δ(Xᵢ) is directly observable from unlabeled snapshots — no labels needed. Better yet, μₚ satisfies a PDE that is linear in V and Φ. This transforms the N! combinatorial matching problem into a functional optimization.

Why the Weak Form?

The particle distribution μₚ evolves according to a Fokker-Planck-type PDE. But μₚ is a sum of delta functions — we can't take its derivatives directly. The weak form solves this: multiply the PDE by a smooth test function ψ and integrate. Now everything becomes empirical averages over particle positions — directly computable from snapshots, with no derivatives of μₚ needed.

Strong Form

Requires ∇μ — impossible for delta functions

Weak Form

Only needs ∫ψ dμ = (1/N)∑ψ(Xᵢ) — computable!

Key Property

Linear in V and Φ → convex optimization

The crucial advantage: because the weak form PDE is linear in V and Φ, we can choose the test function ψ to be V and Φ themselves. This self-testing trick — using the unknowns as their own test functions — produces a loss with a remarkable property.

The Self-Test Loss

Applying Ito's formula with ψ = V + Φ*μ (the energy functional) as the test function, the weak form residual decomposes into three computable terms. Each term is an empirical average over particle snapshots — no velocity estimation, no trajectory matching.

L(θ)=12 Jdiss Δt  −  σ22 Jdiff Δt  +  δEtot\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\, J_{\text{diss}}\, \Delta t \;-\; \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\, J_{\text{diff}}\, \Delta t \;+\; \delta E_{\text{tot}}L(θ)=21​Jdiss​Δt−2σ2​Jdiff​Δt+δEtot​

Anatomy of the Loss

Each term comes from the weak form PDE tested against the energy functional:

L=12 Jdiss Δt⏟dissipation  −  σ22 Jdiff Δt⏟diffusion  +  δEtot⏟energy change\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\, J_{\text{diss}}\, \Delta t}_{\color{#ef4444}\text{dissipation}} \;-\; \underbrace{\tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\, J_{\text{diff}}\, \Delta t}_{\color{#3b82f6}\text{diffusion}} \;+\; \underbrace{\delta E_{\text{tot}}}_{\color{#f59e0b}\text{energy change}}L=dissipation21​Jdiss​Δt​​−diffusion2σ2​Jdiff​Δt​​+energy changeδEtot​​​

Jdiss = Σ‖drift‖² / N

Dissipation: how much work the drift forces do. The 1/2 coefficient (from the self-test trick) is what breaks the degeneracy — it makes L(V*, Φ*) strictly negative.

Jdiff = (1/N) ΣiΔV + (1/N²) Σi≠jΔΦ

Diffusion contribution: depends on the Laplacian of V and Φ. Computable from snapshots via the basis expansion or neural network parameterization.

δEtot = E(t+Δt) − E(t)

Energy change between two snapshots. Directly observable from unlabeled data — no trajectory information needed!

E[L(V∗,Φ∗)]=−12 E[Jdiss∗ Δt]<0vs.L(0,0)=0\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{L}(V^*, \Phi^*)] = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}[J_{\text{diss}}^*\,\Delta t] < 0 \qquad \text{vs.} \qquad \mathcal{L}(0, 0) = 0E[L(V∗,Φ∗)]=−21​E[Jdiss∗​Δt]<0vs.L(0,0)=0

The true potentials produce a strictly negative loss, while the trivial solution gives zero — this breaks the degeneracy that plagues squared-residual approaches.

Why Not Just Square the Residual?

Squaring the weak form residual creates a degenerate minimum at V=0, Φ=0 (the trivial solution). Our self-test loss, by using V and Φ as their own test functions, has a strictly negative minimum at the true potentials.

Why Not Energy Balance?

You might wonder: since the system conserves energy, couldn't we just match the dissipation and diffusion terms directly? This is mathematically valid — for the true potentials, the energy identity does hold. But there's a fatal problem: using this for parameter estimation requires matching |∇V|² and |∇Φ|² terms, which are 4th-order in the potentials. The resulting optimization landscape is non-convex and computationally intractable.

Energy Balance Approach

Match dissipation = diffusion − δE. Involves |∇V|² and |∇Φ|² → 4th-order equations. Non-convex, intractable.

Our Self-Test (Variation Approach)

Test the weak form PDE against ψ = V + Φ*μ. Only 2nd-order terms. Linear in basis coefficients → convex, solvable.

The demo below illustrates the energy identity: when V and Φ are correct (both scales = 1), the three terms cancel. But turning this into an estimator would require 4th-order optimization — our self-test achieves the same goal with only 2nd-order terms.

Two Implementations

From Theory to Practice

The self-test loss is agnostic to how V and Φ are parameterized. We implement it two ways:

📐

Self-Test LSE

Expand V, Φ in known polynomial/exponential basis functions. Solve via linear least squares — exact, fast, but requires prior knowledge of the potential form.

✓ Exact solution
× Needs known basis
🧠

Self-Test NN

Parameterize V, Φ with MLPs. Optimize via gradient descent — flexible, no prior knowledge needed, but requires training.

✓ No prior knowledge
× Requires training

↓ Same self-test loss ↓

The loss is our contribution, not the parameterization. Both implementations share the same theoretical guarantee.

Theory Validation

Error Bound & Scaling

∣θ^M,Δt−θ∗∣  ≤  C ⁣((Δt)α⏟discretization+1M⏟statistical)|\widehat{\theta}_{M,\Delta t} - \theta_*| \;\le\; C\!\left(\underbrace{(\Delta t)^\alpha}_{\text{discretization}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}}_{\text{statistical}}\right)∣θM,Δt​−θ∗​∣≤C​discretization(Δt)α​​+statisticalM​1​​​​

More snapshots M → statistical error drops as 1/√M. The discretization bias depends on the quadrature rule: α=1 for Riemann sum (O(Δt) floor), α=2 for trapezoidal/chordwise rule (O((Δt)²) floor). See Figure 2 below for the comparison.

Figure 2: M-scaling — Riemann vs Trapezoidal

This figure validates the error bound formula above. As the number of snapshots M increases, both ∇V and ∇Φ errors decrease — but they eventually hit a floor determined by the quadrature rule.

Left pair: Riemann sum (α=1)

Error drops as 1/√M, then saturates at O(Δt) — the Riemann discretization floor. Adding more data doesn't help.

Right pair: Trapezoidal rule (α=2)

Same 1/√M statistical rate, but the floor drops to O((Δt)²) — quadratically better. This is why we use the trapezoidal rule.

Figure 3: Discrete-Model Bias

What happens when you observe data at the same resolution you simulate? When the fine simulation step δt equals the observation gap Δt (zero gap), there is no room to refine the quadrature. The O(Δt) bias becomes intrinsic to the discrete model itself.

Takeaway: the error floor in this regime is a fundamental limit of the discrete model, not a numerical artifact. To push accuracy below O(Δt), you must use finer simulation (δt < Δt) and a higher-order quadrature rule.

Regularization

The L-Curve: Automatic Regularization

Solving the basis regression (A + λI)x = b requires choosing the regularization parameter λ. Too small → noise amplified. Too large → solution over-smoothed. The L-curve method finds the optimal λ at the point of maximum curvature on the log-log trade-off curve between solution norm and residual norm.

The L-curve's characteristic ‘L’ shape emerges from two regimes: the vertical arm (small λ, noise dominates) and the horizontal arm (large λ, regularization bias dominates). The corner is the sweet spot. Try increasing the condition number to see how ill-conditioning sharpens the L-curve.

The key innovation: the (1/2) coefficient on the dissipation term means L(V*, Φ*) = -(1/2) E[J_diss Δt] < 0, while L(0,0) = 0. This breaks the trivial-solution degeneracy.

Neural Network Architecture

Both V and Φ are parameterized by 3-layer MLPs with [64, 64, 64] hidden dimensions. The activation function must be C²-smooth (e.g. Softplus or Tanh) — ReLU is not allowed because the self-test loss requires computing Laplacians via automatic differentiation, which needs second derivatives to exist. The symmetry Φ(x) = Φ(-x) is enforced architecturally. Total: ~17,000 parameters.
